Algebra? Isn't it just $abc$? How hard could it be? If that's what you think, you'd be wrong. Elementary algebra often leaves us with the illusion that algebra is nothing more than "using letters to represent numbers" — but the true meaning of algebra is "using symbols to represent relations."

Take the familiar example of letters standing in for numbers. When we first learn arithmetic, everyone knows $1+2=2+1$, $3+4=4+3$. Replacing the numbers on both sides with letters, the form $a+b=b+a$ hints at something deeper: from a higher, more abstract vantage point, we can uncover the hidden relations buried within a structure.

Abstraction

Abstraction is the heart of algebra. Through abstraction, we free ourselves from specific numerical values and focus instead on the relations and structures between them. This allows us to discover more general laws and theorems, rather than being confined to particular numbers. Going further, abstraction lets us connect different areas of mathematics — algebra with geometry, algebra with number theory, and so on. By abstracting, we find similar structures and relations across different mathematical domains, gaining a deeper understanding of what mathematics really is.

The abstraction of numbers is something most people find familiar. From the natural numbers to the integers, then to the rationals, the reals, and the complex numbers — each step is an act of abstraction. By introducing new elements and rules of operation, we become able to solve more problems: negative numbers let us handle subtraction that yields a negative result, and complex numbers let us handle quadratic equations with no real solution. In deeper mathematics — group theory, ring theory, field theory, and the like — this abstraction of numbers reaches a new level. In these fields, the objects we abstract are "structures," and the "relations" between structures are far richer than the relations between numbers such as equality, greater than, or less than. Examples include mappings between sets, homomorphisms between groups, continuous maps between topological spaces, and so on.

Alexander Grothendieck, the father of algebraic geometry, argued that these relations between structures are the true core of algebra. By studying these relations, we can discover deeper mathematical laws and theorems, and understand the essence of mathematics more fully. In algebraic geometry, by studying maps between algebraic varieties, we uncover their properties and structure. In group theory, by studying homomorphisms between groups, we reveal what a group truly is. In topology, continuous maps between topological spaces tell us the nature of those spaces. We also find that many intrinsic properties of a structure can be characterized entirely by its external relations to other structures — this leads to the notions of universal properties and the Yoneda lemma, which belong to category theory. A category is the "structure of structures and relations," and it allows us to look at our mathematics from an even higher dimension.

Why Do We Need Algebra?

Algebra is arguably one of the purest and most important branches of mathematics. The more abstract a theory is, the more universally it tends to apply. The work of an algebraist is to make "nontrivial conclusions" become "trivial" through abstraction and structure. You might feel that describing mathematical objects (such as numbers, functions, or spaces) with certain structures (such as groups, rings, schemes, or categories) is "too abstract" — but it is precisely this abstraction and structuring that lets us discover deeper laws and theorems, and see things that naive methods simply cannot.
