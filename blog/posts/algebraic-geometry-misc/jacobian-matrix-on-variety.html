(From Ravi Vakil, The Rising Sea, 13.1.7.)

Let $X$ be a $k$-scheme of finite type. Then locally, $X$ has the form $$\operatorname{Spec}k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$$ Let $p:\operatorname{Spec}k\to X$ be a $k$-point on $X$. The Zariski tangent space at $p$ is defined as $T_pX:=\mathfrak{m}/\mathfrak{m}^2$, where $\mathfrak{m}$ is the maximal ideal at $p$.

For the scheme $X$ and $k$-point $p$, we define formal partial derivatives by $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(ax_i^n)=nax_i^{n-1}$$ and define the Jacobian matrix $$J_p=\begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1}(p)\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n}(p) \end{pmatrix}$$ This corresponds to a linear map $k^n\to k^r$. We have the following theorem.

Let $A=B/I$. Then $A^2\simeq (B^2+I)/I$, where $A^2$ and $B^2$ denote the square ideals of $A$ and $B$ respectively. In $B/I$, the product of two elements is $(x+I)(y+I)=xy+I$. Since every element of $A$ has the form $x+I$ for $x\in B$, every element of $A^2$ has the form $xy+I$ for $x,y\in B$, and clearly $xy\in B^2$. Elements of $B^2+I$ have the form $x+y$ where $x\in B^2, y\in I$, so every element of $A^2$ has the form $x+I$ for $x\in B^2+I$. Therefore $A^2\simeq (B^2+I)/I$. The cokernel of the above linear map is the tangent space $T_pX$. That is, $\operatorname{coker} J_p\simeq\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$. To simplify notation, we may assume that $p$ is the origin of affine space $\mathbb A^n_k$. Indeed, if $p=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$ is not the origin, we apply the change of variables $y_i=x_i-a_i$. One can verify the polynomial ring isomorphism $$k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\simeq k[y_1,\ldots,y_n]$$ and that partial derivatives are invariant under translation: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(p)=\frac{\partial}{\partial y_i}(0)$$ So without loss of generality, the assumption is justified. We set up notation and algebraic relations:
  • The ambient polynomial ring $R=k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$.
  • Let $I=(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$, so the local structure of $X$ is given by the quotient ring $R/I$.
  • The maximal ideal of the origin $p=(0,\ldots,0)$ is $\mathfrak n=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$.
  • The coordinate ring of $X$ is $A=R/I$.
The point $p$ lies on $X$ if and only if $f_i(p)=0$ for all $i$. Since $p$ is the origin, this forces the constant term of each $f_i$ to vanish, so $I\subset\mathfrak n$. The maximal ideal of $p$ in $A$ is therefore $\mathfrak m=\mathfrak n/I$, and the Zariski tangent space is $T_pX=\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$.
Our task is to compute $\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$. By Lemma 1, $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2=(\mathfrak n/I)/((\mathfrak n^2+I)/I)\tag{I}$$ By the third isomorphism theorem for rings, $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2\simeq \mathfrak n/(\mathfrak n^2+I)$$ We know that $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ is a $k$-vector space spanned by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$, which we identify with $k^n$. The quotient $\mathfrak n/(\mathfrak n^2+I)$ is then $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ modulo the subspace generated by the images of elements of $I$. We now examine how elements of $I$ map into $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$. For any $f_j\in I$, since $f_j(0)=0$, we have the Taylor expansion $$f_j=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_i}(0)x_i+\text{higher order terms}$$ When we map $f_j$ into $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$, the higher order terms vanish since they lie in $\mathfrak n^2$. So the image of $f_j$ is its linear part $$\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_i}(0)x_i$$ which corresponds to the column vector in $k^n$ (with basis $x_1,\ldots,x_n$): $$\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_1}(0)\\ \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_n}(0)\end{pmatrix}$$ This is precisely the $j$-th column of the Jacobian matrix $J_p$.
Since $I$ is generated by $f_1,\ldots,f_r$, the image of $I$ in $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ is the subspace spanned by the columns of $J_p$, i.e., the image $(\mathfrak n^2+I)/\mathfrak n^2$ of $I$ under $\mathfrak n\to\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$. Recalling (I), $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2\simeq \frac{\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2}{(\mathfrak n^2+I)/\mathfrak n^2}\simeq k^n/\operatorname{im} J_p=\operatorname{coker}J_p$$
