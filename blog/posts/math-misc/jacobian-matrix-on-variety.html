(From Ravi Vakil, The Rising Sea, 13.1.7.)

Let $X$ be a $k$-scheme of finite type. Then locally, $X$ has the form $$\operatorname{Spec}k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$$ Let $p:\operatorname{Spec}k\to X$ be a $k$-point on $X$. The Zariski tangent space at $p$ is defined as $T_pX:=\mathfrak{m}/\mathfrak{m}^2$, where $\mathfrak{m}$ is the maximal ideal at $p$.

For the scheme $X$ and $k$-point $p$, we define formal partial derivatives by $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(ax_i^n)=nax_i^{n-1}$$ and define the Jacobian matrix $$J_p=\begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1}(p)\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n}(p) \end{pmatrix}$$ This corresponds to a linear map $k^n\to k^r$. We have the following theorem.

Let $A=B/I$. Then $A^2\simeq (B^2+I)/I$, where $A^2$ and $B^2$ denote the square ideals of $A$ and $B$ respectively. In $B/I$, the product of two elements is $(x+I)(y+I)=xy+I$. Since every element of $A$ has the form $x+I$ for $x\in B$, every element of $A^2$ has the form $xy+I$ for $x,y\in B$, and clearly $xy\in B^2$. Elements of $B^2+I$ have the form $x+y$ where $x\in B^2, y\in I$, so every element of $A^2$ has the form $x+I$ for $x\in B^2+I$. Therefore $A^2\simeq (B^2+I)/I$. The cokernel of the above linear map is the tangent space $T_pX$. That is, $\operatorname{coker} J_p\simeq\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$. To simplify notation, we may assume that $p$ is the origin of affine space $\mathbb A^n_k$. Indeed, if $p=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$ is not the origin, we apply the change of variables $y_i=x_i-a_i$. One can verify the polynomial ring isomorphism $$k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]\simeq k[y_1,\ldots,y_n]$$ and that partial derivatives are invariant under translation: $$\frac{\partial}{\partial x_i}(p)=\frac{\partial}{\partial y_i}(0)$$ So without loss of generality, the assumption is justified. We set up notation and algebraic relations:
  • The ambient polynomial ring $R=k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$.
  • Let $I=(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$, so the local structure of $X$ is given by the quotient ring $R/I$.
  • The maximal ideal of the origin $p=(0,\ldots,0)$ is $\mathfrak n=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$.
  • The coordinate ring of $X$ is $A=R/I$.
The point $p$ lies on $X$ if and only if $f_i(p)=0$ for all $i$. Since $p$ is the origin, this forces the constant term of each $f_i$ to vanish, so $I\subset\mathfrak n$. The maximal ideal of $p$ in $A$ is therefore $\mathfrak m=\mathfrak n/I$, and the Zariski tangent space is $T_pX=\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$.
Our task is to compute $\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$. By Lemma 1, $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2=(\mathfrak n/I)/((\mathfrak n^2+I)/I)\tag{I}$$ By the third isomorphism theorem for rings, $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2\simeq \mathfrak n/(\mathfrak n^2+I)$$ We know that $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ is a $k$-vector space spanned by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$, which we identify with $k^n$. The quotient $\mathfrak n/(\mathfrak n^2+I)$ is then $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ modulo the subspace generated by the images of elements of $I$. We now examine how elements of $I$ map into $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$. For any $f_j\in I$, since $f_j(0)=0$, we have the Taylor expansion $$f_j=\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_i}(0)x_i+\text{higher order terms}$$ When we map $f_j$ into $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$, the higher order terms vanish since they lie in $\mathfrak n^2$. So the image of $f_j$ is its linear part $$\sum_{i=1}^n\frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_i}(0)x_i$$ which corresponds to the column vector in $k^n$ (with basis $x_1,\ldots,x_n$): $$\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_1}(0)\\ \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_j}{\partial x_n}(0)\end{pmatrix}$$ This is precisely the $j$-th column of the Jacobian matrix $J_p$.
Since $I$ is generated by $f_1,\ldots,f_r$, the image of $I$ in $\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$ is the subspace spanned by the columns of $J_p$, i.e., the image $(\mathfrak n^2+I)/\mathfrak n^2$ of $I$ under $\mathfrak n\to\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2$. Recalling (I), $$\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2\simeq \frac{\mathfrak n/\mathfrak n^2}{(\mathfrak n^2+I)/\mathfrak n^2}\simeq k^n/\operatorname{im} J_p=\operatorname{coker}J_p$$

Note that in most contexts, the Jacobian matrix is usually the transpose of the matrix above (but here we work with the cotangent space, hence column vectors).

Jacobian Corank Function

Let $A$ be a finitely generated $k$-algebra generated by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ with ideal generated by $f_1,\ldots,f_r$, and let $p\in\operatorname{Spec} A$. For a polynomial $g\in I$, form the gradient vector $$\nabla g(p)=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_1}(p)\\ \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}(p)\end{pmatrix}$$ Appending this gradient vector to the Jacobian matrix yields a new matrix $$\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1}(p) & \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_1}(p)\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n}(p) & \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_n}(p)\end{pmatrix}$$ This new matrix has the same rank as the Jacobian matrix. In other words, the corank of the matrix at $p$ does not depend on the choice of generators $f_1,\ldots,f_r$. Since $g\in I=(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$, we can write $g=\sum_{i=1}^r a_i f_i$. Differentiating by the product rule: $$\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_j}=\sum_{i=1}^r\left(\frac{\partial a_i}{\partial x_j}f_i+a_i\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\right)\tag{II}$$ Since $p\in\operatorname{Spec} A$, we have $f_i(p)=0$ for all $i$. Evaluating (II) at $p$: $$\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_j}(p)=\sum_{i=1}^r a_i(p)\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(p)$$ Combining over all variables $x_j$: $$\nabla g(p)=\sum_{i=1}^r a_i(p)\nabla f_i(p)$$ This shows $\nabla g(p)$ is a linear combination of the columns of $J_p$, so the augmented matrix has the same rank as $J_p$. Let $A$ be a finitely generated $k$-algebra generated by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ with ideal generated by $f_1,\ldots,f_r$, and let $p\in\operatorname{Spec} A$. Let $q(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]$, and set $h=y-q(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\in k[x_1,\ldots,x_n,y]$. Then the Jacobian matrix of $(f_1,\ldots,f_r,h)$ with respect to variables $x_1,\ldots,x_n,y$ at $p$ has the same rank as the Jacobian matrix of $(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$ with respect to $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ at $p$. In other words, in this setting, the rank of the Jacobian matrix does not depend on the choice of generators. The expanded matrix is: $$J'=\begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1} & \dots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x_1} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n} & \dots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x_n} \\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial y} & \dots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial y} & \dfrac{\partial h}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix}$$ Observing the entries involving the new variable $y$ and new equation $h$:
  • Since each $f_i$ involves only $x_1,\ldots,x_n$, we have $\dfrac{\partial f_i}{\partial y}=0$.
  • Since $h=y-q(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$, we have $\dfrac{\partial h}{\partial y}=1$.
  • Since $h=y-q(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$, we have $\dfrac{\partial h}{\partial x_j}=-\dfrac{\partial q}{\partial x_j}$.
Substituting into $J'$: $$J'(p)=\begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(p) & \dots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1}(p) & -\dfrac{\partial q}{\partial x_1}(p) \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(p) & \dots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n}(p) & -\dfrac{\partial q}{\partial x_n}(p) \\ 0 & \dots & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$ By elementary row operations, $\operatorname{rank} J'(p)=\operatorname{rank} J(p)+1$. The coranks are $n-\operatorname{rank} J(p)$ and $n+1-\operatorname{rank} J'(p)$ respectively, which are clearly equal. Every $k$-scheme $X$ of finite type carries a function from the underlying set of $X$ to $\Z^{\ge 0}$, called the Jacobian corank function. Its value at a $k$-point $p$ is $\dim_k T_pX$. We denote it $\mathrm{cork}:X\to\Z^{\ge 0}$; it is the rank of the coherent sheaf of differentials $\Omega_{X/k}$.

Recall that a function $f:X\to\Z^{\ge 0}$ on a topological space $X$ is upper semicontinuous if for every $n\in\Z^{\ge 0}$, the set $\{x\in X:f(x)\ge n\}$ is closed. An important property of upper semicontinuous functions is that they always attain their maximum. The Jacobian corank satisfies this:

Let $X$ be a $k$-scheme of finite type. Then the Jacobian corank function $\mathrm{cork}:X\to\Z^{\ge 0}$ is upper semicontinuous. In particular, $\mathrm{cork}$ attains its maximum. Upper semicontinuity is a local property, so we may assume $X$ is affine, generated by $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ with ideal generated by $f_1,\ldots,f_r$. The value of $\mathrm{cork}$ at $p$ is the corank of $J_p$. For any $n\in\Z^{\ge 0}$, the set $\{p\in X:\mathrm{cork}(p)\ge n\}=\{p\in X:\operatorname{rank} J_p\le n\}$ is closed. $\mathrm{cork}$ is preserved under field extension. That is, if $X$ is a $k$-scheme of finite type and $\ell/k$ is a field extension, then under the base change $$X\times_k\ell\to X,\quad p\mapsto q$$ we always have $\mathrm{cork}(p)=\mathrm{cork}(q)$. We may reduce to the affine case. Let $X$ be affine with coordinate ring $A=k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$. After base change $\ell/k$, the corresponding affine open has coordinate ring $$A_\ell=A\otimes_k\ell\simeq\ell[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$$ Note that $f_1,\ldots,f_r$ have coefficients in $k$ but are viewed as polynomials with $\ell$-coefficients in $A_\ell$. Let $\pi:X_\ell\to X$ be the base change map, take $p\in X$, and set $q=\pi(p)$. Then:
  • The point $q$ corresponds to a prime ideal $\mathfrak q$ of $A$ with residue field $\kappa(q)$.
  • The point $p$ corresponds to a prime ideal $\mathfrak p$ of $A_\ell$ with residue field $\kappa(p)$.
This induces a natural field extension $\kappa(q)\hookrightarrow\kappa(p)$. We form Jacobian matrices $J_q$ and $J_p$ at $q$ and $p$ respectively:
  • At $q\in X$: entries of $J_q$ are $\dfrac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}$ evaluated in $\kappa(q)$.
  • At $p\in X_\ell$: entries of $J_p$ are $\dfrac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}$ evaluated in $\kappa(p)$.
Since the coefficients of each $f_i$ lie in $k$, each entry of $J_p$ is the image of the corresponding entry of $J_q$ under $\kappa(q)\hookrightarrow\kappa(p)$. By elementary linear algebra, $J_p$ and $J_q$ have the same rank, hence $\mathrm{cork}(p)=\mathrm{cork}(q)$.

The following is an important application of this theory, which allows us to determine whether a point is a regular point of a scheme.

Let $X=\operatorname{Spec}k[x_1,\ldots,x_n]/(f_1,\ldots,f_r)$ be a $k$-scheme of finite type of pure dimension $d$. Then a $k$-point $p$ is a regular point of $X$ if and only if $\mathrm{cork}(p)=d$. We may assume $p$ is the origin, corresponding to maximal ideal $\mathfrak m=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$. For each generator $f_i$ of $I$, Taylor expand at the origin: $$f_i=\sum_{j=1}^n\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(p)x_j+\text{higher order terms}$$ Since $f_i(p)=0$ and higher order terms lie in $\mathfrak m^2$, the image of $f_i$ in $\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$ is its linear part: $$\overline f_i=\sum_{j=1}^n\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}(p)\overline x_j$$ where $\{\overline x_1,\ldots,\overline x_n\}$ is the basis of $\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$. The coordinates of $\overline f_i$ in this basis form the $i$-th row of: $$J_p=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(p)\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_1}(p) & \cdots & \dfrac{\partial f_r}{\partial x_n}(p)\end{pmatrix}$$ The subspace $(I+\mathfrak m^2)/\mathfrak m^2$ of $\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2$ is spanned by $\overline f_1,\ldots,\overline f_r$, so by linear algebra: $$\dim_k\frac{I+\mathfrak m^2}{\mathfrak m^2}=\operatorname{rk}J_p$$ The local ring $\mathscr O_{X,p}$ has maximal ideal $\overline{\mathfrak m}=\mathfrak m/I$ with cotangent space $\overline{\mathfrak m}/\overline{\mathfrak m}^2$. We have: $$\overline{\mathfrak m}/\overline{\mathfrak m}^2\simeq\frac{\mathfrak m/I}{(\mathfrak m^2+I)/I}\simeq\frac{\mathfrak m}{\mathfrak m^2+I}$$ From the short exact sequence: $$0\to\frac{I+\mathfrak m^2}{\mathfrak m^2}\to\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2\to\frac{\mathfrak m}{\mathfrak m^2+I}\to 0$$ we obtain the dimension formula: $$\dim_k\overline{\mathfrak m}/\overline{\mathfrak m}^2=\dim_k\mathfrak m/\mathfrak m^2-\dim_k\frac{I+\mathfrak m^2}{\mathfrak m^2}=n-\operatorname{rk}J_p$$ Therefore $\mathrm{cork}(p)=\dim_k\overline{\mathfrak m}/\overline{\mathfrak m}^2$.
