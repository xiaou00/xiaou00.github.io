A ring — a simple object, though of course we are not talking about the kind you wear on your finger. In mathematics, a ring is an important algebraic structure equipped with addition and multiplication that satisfies the distributive law. The concept of a ring plays a central role in abstract algebra: it is the prototype of almost all algebraic operations, more general than a field, and capable of representing a broader class of mathematical objects.

A ring is a set $(R,+,\cdot)$ equipped with two operations — addition and multiplication — satisfying the following conditions:
  • Abelian group axioms. $R$ is an abelian group under addition (details omitted).
  • Associativity of multiplication. For all $a,b,c \in R$, $(a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c)$.
  • Distributive laws. For all $a,b,c \in R$, $a \cdot (b + c) = a \cdot b + a \cdot c$ and $(a + b) \cdot c = a \cdot c + b \cdot c$.
We write $0$ for the additive identity of the ring, $-a$ for the additive inverse of $a$, and $1$ for the multiplicative identity (if one exists).

The elements of a ring can be numbers, polynomials, matrices, functions, or anything else that satisfies the axioms above. The notion of a ring appears throughout mathematics, including number theory, algebraic geometry, and quantum mechanics in physics.

Types of Rings

Rings can be classified by additional properties they may satisfy:

  • Commutative ring: If multiplication in $R$ is commutative, i.e., $a \cdot b = b \cdot a$ for all $a,b \in R$, then $R$ is called a commutative ring.
  • Unital ring: If $R$ has a multiplicative identity $1$, then $R$ is called a unital ring.
  • Division ring: A unital ring in which every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. The quaternions $\mathbb{H}$ are a classic example of a division ring that is not a field.
  • Domain: A ring with no zero divisors, i.e., if $a \cdot b = 0$ then $a = 0$ or $b = 0$ for all $a,b \in R$.
  • Integral domain: A commutative ring with no zero divisors. The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ are a prototypical example.
  • Field: A commutative ring in which every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$, reals $\mathbb{R}$, and complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ are all fields.

Examples of Rings

The integers $\mathbb{Z}$ form a fundamental ring, with the usual integer addition and multiplication. It is a commutative, unital ring, but not a field, since no integer other than $1$ and $-1$ has a multiplicative inverse.

The polynomial ring $R[x]$ (where $R$ is itself a ring) consists of polynomials in the variable $x$, with the usual polynomial addition and multiplication. If $R$ is a field, then $R[x]$ is a commutative ring but not a field, since polynomials of positive degree have no multiplicative inverse. If $R$ is commutative, so is $R[x]$.

The quotient ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (notation to be explained later) consists of congruence classes of integers modulo $n$, with addition and multiplication defined modulo $n$. For example, $\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z}$ is a field because every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse, whereas $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is not, since $2$ and $3$ have no inverses. It is a field if and only if $n$ is prime.
